Diabetes Mellitus Overview

Diabetes mellitus is a chronic metabolic disorder characterized by elevated blood glucose levels. There are primarily two types of diabetes:

Type 1 Diabetes:
- An autoimmune condition where the pancreas produces little or no insulin
- Usually diagnosed in children and young adults
- Requires insulin therapy for management
- Accounts for 5-10% of all diabetes cases

Type 2 Diabetes:
- The body becomes resistant to insulin or doesn't produce enough insulin
- Most common form, accounting for 90-95% of cases
- Often associated with obesity, physical inactivity, and genetic factors
- Can be managed with lifestyle changes, oral medications, or insulin

Symptoms of Diabetes:
- Increased thirst and frequent urination
- Extreme hunger
- Unexplained weight loss
- Fatigue
- Blurred vision
- Slow-healing sores
- Frequent infections

Diagnosis:
- Fasting blood glucose test: 126 mg/dL or higher indicates diabetes
- HbA1c test: 6.5% or higher indicates diabetes
- Oral glucose tolerance test

Management:
- Blood glucose monitoring
- Healthy diet (low in simple carbohydrates)
- Regular physical activity
- Medications as prescribed
- Insulin therapy when needed
- Regular check-ups with healthcare provider

Complications:
- Cardiovascular disease
- Nerve damage (neuropathy)
- Kidney damage (nephropathy)
- Eye damage (retinopathy)
- Foot damage
- Skin conditions
- Alzheimer's disease (potential link)

Prevention (Type 2):
- Maintain a healthy weight
- Stay physically active
- Eat a balanced diet
- Avoid smoking
- Limit alcohol consumption
